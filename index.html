How increased campus safety measures has potentially negative impacts on students' well-being

Students should feel safe at school, not scared of it.

Portfolio

About

With the influx of mass school shootings in the United States, schools are challenged to balance a supportive environment with a safe one. After experiencing this crisis first-hand at Michigan State University, we realized that increased safety measures can foster a hostile environment for students, furthering their trauma from such events. Increased campus safety measures such as police presence, metal detectors, alarms, notifications, or surveillance cameras are necessary for schools, but we are concerned about the well-being of students with these measures in their everyday surroundings.

The United States is facing a crisis, with over 103 daily gun deaths and over 58 fatal school shootings since 2013. With safety procedures being a priority in the U.S. school system today, our schools cannot risk sacrificing a safe, comforting, and encouraging environment for students.

Increasing school security includes metal detectors, closing campuses for lunch, random dog sniffs, random contraband sweeps, drug testing, uniform requirements, clear book bag requirements; all of these things help increase safety for students but they also make students feel uncomfortable and without privacy which will eventually affect their grades and motivation at school.

According to the article, schools with increased security show higher rates of suspension, African American students are 4 times more likely to be involved in high surveillance schools and also have the highest suspension rates. Due to high levels of surveillance, students are more likely to have lower math test scores and less likely to enroll in college because they fear the same levels of surveillance will carry on in the next chapter of their lives.

Academic and Behavioral Consequences of Visible Security Measures in Schools


In this video, Emily Tanner-Smith presents her research with Peabody Research Institute to describe U.S. “academic and behavioral consequences of visible security measures in schools.” Although there is a large quantity of useful information in this piece of media, we suggest to watch from 20:50 to 31:52 to get the most from the provided data.

In summary, Tanner-Smith uses statistical data gathered from student and school surveys to demonstrate the effects that on-campus security has on students. In particular, she targets schools with metal detectors, security cameras, and security personnel to determine the changes in students' behavioral and academic performance. The data she gathered was presented visually through graphs to show how most students performed sizably worse with security on the premises. It is clear that her research is pessimistic about the future of school security and shows an evident decline in student behavior and academic achievement.

How much is too much?

Click here to open this PDF in a new window

The security measures that are put into place after a mass shooting occurs are necessary to ensure the safety of all involved but can be detrimental to a student's education and mental health because of an overabundance of protocols and being surrounded by reminders that can induce stress and anxiety. A Sanford study found that school shootings are linked to higher rates of depression and suicide in students exposed, and negative academic effects as well. Following the shootings we experienced here at MSU, there was a call for more security and a more protected campus. With this though, how much security was too much for everyone involved? The Oxford shooting brought on security measures such as police dogs, metal detectors, and much more. When there is this much security though, there is bound to be an overabundance for a student who is going to school for education, rather than constant worry and fear. This year, the governor added an additional $29 million to the mental health budget in Michigan schools, and so that is a positive and proactive way to ensure some safety in schools. Though being prepared to stay secure and ready can be found to have beneficial results, there needs to be a change in how security is approached in schools and think about the mental strain and effects on students.

Campus safety failing to meet student mental health needs infographic

Strengthening school security measures has become a common preventive response to school violence. However, using security measures, such as metal detectors and digital surveillance technology, had a negative impact on students' perceptions of school safety. It is possible that metal detectors inconvenience students and that they do not like the feeling of being searched and monitored by security devices.

The mean prevalence of the students who felt unsafe at school was 19.4% and ranged from 6.1% to 69.1%. Not feeling safe at school was related to being victimized and mental health difficulties, including depressive symptoms and suicidal behavior.
